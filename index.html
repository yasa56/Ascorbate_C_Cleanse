Practical Tips
• Dissolve each dose in 2+ oz water. Room temperature is best for absorption.
• Allow effervescence to abate (~2 min) before drinking.
• Batch option: dissolve up to 10 tsp (30 g) in 10–20 oz water; store in capped dark bottle; stable 1 day if cool & sealed.
• Hot or burning stools: apply calendula ointment externally.
• To stop ascorbate: taper gradually — do not stop suddenly.
• First session: plan to stay home most of the day (3–8+ hours possible).

Molecular Biochemistry
Reduced Form
L-Ascorbate (AA)
The biologically active, electron-rich form. Monoanion at physiological pH. Primary antioxidant in plasma and cytoplasm.
Oxidized Form
Dehydroascorbate (DHA)
Reversibly oxidized. Transported via GLUT1/3. Rapidly recycled back to AA by glutathione and thioredoxin reductase.
Ascorbate is a unique antioxidant because it donates a single electron rather than two. This produces the ascorbyl free radical (monodehydroascorbate) — a notably stable, unreactive species that preferentially dismutates rather than propagating oxidative chain reactions. This chemical behavior makes ascorbate a terminal antioxidant: it stops radical chains without generating dangerous intermediates.
Ascorbate Recycling Pathway:
AA → Ascorbyl Radical → DHA → AA (via glutathione/thioredoxin reductase)
This loop preserves vitamin C at minimal metabolic cost.
Physiology & Transport
Humans, guinea pigs, and most primates lack the enzyme L-gulonolactone oxidase (GULO) — the final enzyme in a 6-step glucose-to-ascorbate biosynthesis pathway. Without it, we depend entirely on dietary intake. Most mammals synthesize 10–30 g/day endogenously; under stress, production more than doubles.
Intestinal Absorption — Dual Transporter System
SVCT1
Located on apical brush-border of intestinal epithelium and renal tubules. High-capacity, Na⁺-coupled (2:1 ratio). Governs whole-body ascorbate homeostasis and prevents renal loss. Expression is downregulated when plasma levels are high.
SVCT2
Ubiquitously expressed — brain, retina, placenta, adrenal glands, neurons. High-affinity, low-capacity. Protects metabolically active tissues from oxidative stress. Essential for brain development (Slc23a2 knockout mice show high perinatal mortality).
GLUT1/3
Transports the oxidized form DHA. Competed by glucose — high blood glucose reduces vitamin C uptake into cells. Critical in erythrocytes and blood-brain barrier.

Antioxidant Mechanisms
Ascorbate functions as the primary aqueous antioxidant in human plasma, operating more than 100× faster than glutathione at scavenging superoxide radical (O₂•⁻) and singlet oxygen (¹O₂). It is especially critical in the nucleus, which contains little or no superoxide dismutase (SOD) activity.
ROS Scavenging
Directly neutralizes superoxide (O₂•⁻), hydroxyl radical (•OH), singlet oxygen (¹O₂), and hypochlorous acid (HOCl) generated by neutrophils during immune activation.
Vitamin E Regeneration
Ascorbate reduces the tocopheroxyl radical (vitamin E•) back to α-tocopherol at the membrane–aqueous interface, extending lipid-phase antioxidant protection. Without ascorbate, oxidized vitamin E becomes pro-oxidant.
Glutathione Recycling
Ascorbate upregulates glutathione synthesis and spares it from oxidative consumption. Clinical data: high-dose vitamin C increases leukocyte glutathione by up to 50%. This synergy amplifies the entire cellular antioxidant network.
LDL Protection
Ascorbate prevents LDL oxidation in plasma, a critical step in atherogenesis. It must be fully depleted from plasma before LDL lipid peroxidation can begin — making it the first line of cardiovascular defense.
DNA Protection
Eliminates O₂•⁻ in the nucleus where SOD is absent. Prevents 8-hydroxy-deoxyguanosine (8-OHdG) formation — a key oxidative DNA lesion linked to cancer initiation. Also cofactor for TET enzymes involved in epigenetic demethylation.
Enzymatic Cofactor Roles
Ascorbate serves as an essential cofactor for Fe²⁺/2-oxoglutarate–dependent dioxygenases — a family of over 60 enzymes that require ascorbate to keep their iron center in the active Fe²⁺ state. Without ascorbate, iron oxidizes to Fe³⁺ and the enzyme stops working.
Prolyl & Lysyl Hydroxylases
Hydroxylate proline and lysine residues in procollagen — essential for triple-helix stabilization and cross-linking. Deficiency → scurvy: unraveling collagen, bleeding gums, poor wound healing.
Collagen
Dopamine β-Hydroxylase
Converts dopamine → norepinephrine in adrenal medulla and sympathetic neurons. Ascorbate is the electron donor that regenerates the copper cofactor. Links ascorbate to stress response and mood.
Catecholamines
Peptidyl-Glycine α-Amidating Monooxygenase (PAM)
Activates ~50% of neuropeptides and hormones requiring C-terminal amidation — including oxytocin, vasopressin, gastrin, CRH, and calcitonin. Ascorbate is the obligatory electron donor.
Hormones
Carnitine Biosynthesis (γ-Butyrobetaine Hydroxylase)
Two ascorbate-dependent steps required for carnitine synthesis — the mitochondrial shuttle for long-chain fatty acid oxidation. Deficiency causes fatigue and impaired fat metabolism.
Energy
HIF Prolyl Hydroxylases (PHDs) & TET Enzymes
PHDs regulate hypoxia-inducible factor (HIF-1α) — controlling oxygen-sensing, VEGF, and erythropoietin. TET enzymes control DNA demethylation and epigenetic reprogramming. Ascorbate is essential for both.

Mineral & Vitamin Interactions
Iron — The Most Clinically Significant Interaction
Ascorbate dramatically enhances non-heme iron absorption through two mechanisms: reduction of Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺ (the form transported by DMT-1) and chelation — forming a soluble Fe–ascorbate complex that resists alkalinization in the duodenum. Ascorbate can overcome all dietary inhibitors of iron absorption (phytates, polyphenols, calcium) when consumed together.

Heavy Metal Interactions
Ascorbate has documented protective and chelating activity against multiple toxic metals through oxidative stress reduction, glutathione recycling, and direct chelation:
Lead (Pb) Clinically Studied
500–1000 mg/day significantly reduces blood lead levels in humans. Vitamin C reduces urinary lead excretion markers. Less potent than EDTA but additive with it.
Mercury (Hg) Protective
Alleviates mercury toxicity independently of iron. Raises glutathione levels which directly chelates methylmercury. Iron exacerbates mercury toxicity — avoid co-supplementation.
Cadmium (Cd) & Arsenic (As) Indirect via Iron & Glutathione
Cadmium toxicity alleviated via iron metabolism modulation. Ascorbate reduces arsenic-induced oxidative damage and lowers gestational diabetes risk from arsenic/mercury exposure. High vitamin C intake modifies heavy metal–GDM association.
Cardiovascular & Blood Pressure
Ascorbate acts on the cardiovascular system through four converging molecular pathways, making it one of the most pleiotropic cardiovascular nutrients known:
1 · Nitric Oxide (NO) Preservation
Ascorbate upregulates eNOS expression and activity, increases tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) — the essential eNOS cofactor — and directly scavenges O₂•⁻ that degrades NO. Result: more bioavailable NO → vasodilation, reduced blood pressure, improved endothelial function. Acute IV infusion (3 g) significantly lowers BP in hypertensive patients but not normotensive subjects.
2 · Collagen Synthesis & Arterial Integrity
Ascorbate is the obligatory cofactor for type IV collagen synthesis — critical for basement membrane formation, endothelial cell adhesion, and arterial wall elasticity. Promotes endothelial proliferation and inhibits apoptosis under inflammatory conditions. Daily 500 mg lowered systolic BP by ~10% after one month in clinical trials.
3 · Anti-Inflammatory Pathways (NF-κB & Nrf2)
Inhibits NF-κB — the master inflammatory transcription factor — reducing ICAM-1, VCAM-1, and pro-inflammatory cytokine expression. Simultaneously activates Nrf2, upregulating endogenous antioxidant enzyme expression (HO-1, NQO1). Attenuates post-MI inflammatory injury and improves left ventricular ejection fraction.
4 · LDL Protection & Atherosclerosis Prevention
Plasma ascorbate must be fully depleted before LDL oxidation begins. Ascorbate is the first plasma antioxidant consumed during oxidative stress, acting as a sacrificial protector of LDL. Prevents the initial oxidative step in atherosclerotic plaque formation. Also modulates TGF-β extracellular matrix remodeling and platelet aggregation via collagen-dependent pathways.
Clinical Evidence Summary: Low plasma vitamin C is independently associated with hypertension, endothelial dysfunction, heart disease, atherosclerosis, and stroke. Meta-analyses show supplementation produces modest but consistent reductions in both systolic and diastolic BP, with greatest effect in hypertensive individuals.
Glucose, Insulin & Metabolic Effects
The relationship between ascorbate and glucose metabolism is bidirectional and clinically critical — they compete for the same cellular transporters:
GLUT Transporter Competition
DHA (oxidized vitamin C) enters cells via GLUT1 and GLUT3 — the same transporters used by glucose. High blood glucose competitively inhibits DHA uptake. This means diabetic patients with chronically elevated glucose have chronically reduced intracellular vitamin C — despite adequate dietary intake. Insulin stimulates GLUT4 expression, which may indirectly improve ascorbate availability.
Pancreatic β-Cell Protection
β-cells are highly susceptible to oxidative damage due to low endogenous antioxidant enzyme expression. Ascorbate protects β-cells from ROS-induced apoptosis, preserving insulin secretory capacity. Heavy metal–induced ROS (Hg, As) impairs β-cell function — vitamin C attenuates this pathway.
Insulin Sensitivity & Glucose Homeostasis
Ascorbate reduces oxidative stress–driven insulin resistance. Prevents high glucose–induced endothelial apoptosis in vitro. Sorbitol pathway activation in diabetic hyperglycemia depletes intracellular ascorbate. Some trials show modest fasting glucose reduction with supplementation; effects are more pronounced in type 2 diabetics.
Clinical Implication for Diabetic Patients
Diabetics require higher ascorbate intake to achieve the same intracellular levels as non-diabetics. Serum glucose concentrations must be considered alongside vitamin C levels when assessing status. The buffered mineral l-ascorbate form avoids the acidic pH stress of plain ascorbic acid, making it preferable in metabolically compromised patients.
Important Note: High-dose ascorbate can interfere with glucose oxidase–based blood glucose test strips (false low readings). Patients on continuous glucose monitoring should note this interaction and inform their clinicians.
Immune & Neurological Roles
Immune System
Leukocytes accumulate ascorbate 50–100× above plasma levels — rapidly depleted during infection
Enhances neutrophil chemotaxis, phagocytosis, and oxidative burst; protects neutrophils from self-oxidative damage
Stimulates proliferation and differentiation of T-lymphocytes and natural killer (NK) cells
Interferon-like antiviral activity; reduces duration and severity of viral infections
Neurological System
SVCT2 maintains brain ascorbate at ~10× plasma levels — essential for neurotransmitter synthesis and myelin integrity
Required for dopamine → norepinephrine conversion (dopamine β-hydroxylase cofactor)
Modulates HPA axis; ascorbate depletion in adrenal glands is the earliest sign of psychological stress
TET enzyme cofactor — regulates neuronal DNA methylation, memory consolidation, and stem cell pluripotency
